Background

The Naples metropolitan area and the broader Campania region of southern Italy sit atop one of Europe's most geologically hazardous zones. The area is underlain by the Phlegrean Fields (Campi Flegrei), a sprawling complex of ancient volcanoes that covers a broad swath of the Naples metropolitan area and is both seismically and volcanically active. The ground surface in the Phlegrean Fields has been pushed up 1.3 meters (4.3 feet) since 2006, now standing higher than it was prior to the last major event in 1984, when 40,000 residents were evacuated during a period of intense seismic activity as a precaution against a feared eruption that did not occur.[2]

In the past 10 years, 250 earthquakes with a magnitude of four or higher have occurred within 300 kilometers of Naples, averaging approximately 25 earthquakes per year, or one every 15 days. No earthquakes of magnitude 7 or above have struck near Naples in that period. The strongest recent earthquake near Naples in the past decade occurred on October 30, 2016, with a magnitude of 6.6, centered 244 kilometers north-northwest of Naples at a depth of 8 km. Seismologists have reported a fresh increase in activity around the Phlegrean Fields in recent weeks, and authorities conducted emergency drills the previous summer in preparation for a major event as the frequency of tremors increased.[5][2]

The wider Italian peninsula is one of the most seismically active regions in Europe. Mediterranean seismicity is driven by the collision between the African and Eurasian tectonic plates, and the slow opening of the Tyrrhenian Basin contributes to extension along the Apennine mountain range. A major fault system runs the length of the Apennines, with smaller faults fanning off to the sides. The area around Naples is located within this Apennine zone, which is characterized by strong seismic activity due to the geological structure of the Italian peninsula. Italy's national institute for geophysics and vulcanology has called for a governmental plan to ensure that structures in the at-risk zone can withstand a quake of at least magnitude 5.0, noting that at least 500,000 people live in the zone most at risk should the Phlegrean Fields volcano erupt.[4][8][2]
