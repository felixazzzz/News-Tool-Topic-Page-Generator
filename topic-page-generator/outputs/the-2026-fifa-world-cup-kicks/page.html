2026 FIFA World Cup Opens at Estadio Azteca with Mexico vs. South Africa on June 11

The 2026 FIFA World Cup kicks off on Thursday, June 11, 2026, when Mexico faces South Africa at Estadio Azteca (also known as Estadio Banorte) in Mexico City — making it the first stadium ever to host a FIFA World Cup opening match three times. The tournament, co-hosted by Mexico, Canada, and the United States, will feature 48 teams competing across 104 matches in 16 stadiums, with the final scheduled for July 19 in New York/New Jersey. For the first time in World Cup history, three separate opening ceremonies will be held, one in each host country.

Background

Estadio Azteca — currently operating under a naming rights deal as Estadio Banorte — is the most storied football venue in the Americas and the only stadium in history to host three FIFA World Cups. Opened in 1966 and located in the Coyoacán district of Mexico City at an altitude of 2,200 metres, the stadium previously hosted the 1970 and 1986 FIFA World Cup finals. The 1970 final saw 100,000 fans watch Brazil defeat Italy 4-1, with Pelé playing his third and final World Cup. In 1986, Diego Maradona orchestrated Argentina's 3-2 victory over West Germany in the final, having already scored the famous 'Goal of the Century' against England in the quarter-finals at the same venue.[2][10][12]

Ahead of the 2026 tournament, Estadio Azteca has undergone its first major renovation since its inauguration. The redevelopment — costing around 2 billion Mexican pesos (approximately €110 million) — focused on fan experience, sustainability, and pitch quality. Upgrades include a new roof with photovoltaic panels, full LED lighting on the exterior, high-resolution LED screens throughout the bowl, a hybrid playing surface at FIFA regulation size, new player changing rooms and tunnel, and replaced seating for improved comfort and sightlines. The original stadium silhouette has been preserved but with lighter, more modern materials. The stadium has a tournament capacity of approximately 83,000 and is renowned for its acoustics, with the bowl design trapping and amplifying crowd noise.[2][10][11][12]

The 2026 FIFA World Cup is the largest in the tournament's history, featuring 48 national teams competing across 104 matches in 16 stadiums spread across Canada, Mexico, and the United States. For the first time, three opening ceremonies will be held — one in each host country. The innovative match schedule is designed to minimise travel for teams and fans, with matches grouped into regionalised east, central, and west zones, and a minimum of three days' rest between fixtures for teams in 103 of the 104 matches. Canada, Mexico, and the United States are each guaranteed to play their three group-stage matches on home soil. The tournament final is scheduled for July 19, 2026, in New York/New Jersey.[1][2][9]
