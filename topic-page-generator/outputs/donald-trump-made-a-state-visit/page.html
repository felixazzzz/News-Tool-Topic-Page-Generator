Trump Makes First U.S. Presidential State Visit to China Since 2017, Holding High-Stakes Summit with Xi Jinping

President Donald Trump traveled to Beijing on May 12–15, 2026, for a landmark state visit and summit with Chinese President Xi Jinping at the Great Hall of the People and Zhongnanhai — the first U.S. presidential visit to China since 2017. The two leaders held talks spanning trade, Taiwan, the ongoing Iran war, technology, and energy, culminating in a sweeping trade and economic agreement that included Chinese commitments on rare earth exports, agricultural purchases, and fentanyl precursor controls. The summit also produced a new bilateral framework of 'constructive, strategic stability' and plans for a 'Board of Trade' to oversee ongoing economic commitments.

Background

Trump's May 2026 state visit to Beijing marked the first U.S. presidential visit to China since 2017, when Trump was first in office and Xi staged a 'state visit-plus' that included a private dinner in the Forbidden City, a parade through Tiananmen Square, and a Great Hall ceremony unveiling $250 billion in business deals. The summit had originally been planned for an earlier date but was delayed following U.S. and Israeli military strikes on Iran. For much of 2025, U.S.-China relations were dominated by an escalating trade war in which tariffs reached as high as 145%, with China retaliating by curtailing exports of rare earth minerals and halting purchases of American agricultural exports. A fragile trade truce was reached in October 2025, when Trump and Xi met face-to-face in South Korea, extending the truce for another year and securing a Chinese commitment to purchase 12 million metric tons of U.S. soybeans.[7][9][10][13][14]

Heading into the summit, the two countries faced a complex web of geopolitical tensions spanning trade, Taiwan, the ongoing Iran war, and artificial intelligence. China processes around 90% of the world's rare earth minerals — essential for modern technology from smartphones to jet engines — and had used export controls on these materials as leverage in trade disputes. The U.S. Navy's blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, a consequence of the U.S.-Israel war against Iran, had sent global oil and gas prices soaring, hurting Chinese manufacturers and disrupting supply chains worldwide. Beijing had joined Pakistan as an unofficial mediator in the Iran conflict, presenting a five-point peace plan in March 2026, and China's top priority entering the summit was greater stability and predictability in its relationship with the United States, particularly on tariffs.[9][13][14]

Taiwan remained the most sensitive issue for China throughout the talks. Beijing has been ramping up military pressure by sending warplanes and naval vessels around Taiwan almost daily, and Xi privately warned Trump that differences over Taiwan could bring the U.S. and China into clashes or conflict. The Trump administration had authorized an $11 billion arms package for Taiwan in December 2025 but had not yet moved forward with delivery. Indo-Pacific allies including Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan were watching the summit closely, worried that Trump might make concessions on Taiwan or arms sales in exchange for Chinese support on Iran. A February 2026 Supreme Court decision had also curtailed the president's unilateral tariff power, reshaping the domestic political context for the negotiations.[7][9][13][14]
